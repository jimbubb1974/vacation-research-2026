# Munich — Activities Longlist
*Family of 4 · 2 adults, teens 17 & 14 · ~4 nights in Munich · July 2026*
*Use this list to discuss with the family — far more than 4 days can hold.*

---

## City Sights & Orientation

**Marienplatz & the Glockenspiel**
The heart of Munich. The Glockenspiel chimes daily at 11am, noon, and 5pm with jousting knights and barrel-makers dancing in the tower. Fun to catch once, and the surrounding square is endlessly lively — a good first-morning anchor.

**Asamkirche (Asam Church)**
A tiny baroque church on Sendlinger Strasse built by the Asam brothers as their personal chapel — one of the most ornate interiors in Germany, free, and takes only 20 minutes. The contrast between the plain street outside and the gold-drenched interior is striking. Very easy to add as a walking detour.

**Frauenkirche (Cathedral of Our Lady)**
Munich's most recognizable landmark with the twin onion domes visible across the city. The interior is surprisingly spare but the crypt tour and the legend of the Devil's Footprint give it good story material for teens.

**St. Peter's Church (Alter Peter) — Tower Climb**
The oldest church in Munich, but the reason to go is climbing the 299 steps to the top for the best 360° panorama of the city and Alps. It's tight and a bit breathless, which makes it more fun. €5/adult, €2/youth.

**Viktualienmarkt**
Munich's beloved daily outdoor food market — cheese, bread, meats, beer, seasonal fruit, flower stalls. Good for a mid-morning wander and snack stop. There's a small beer garden right in the middle of the market.

**Maximilianstrasse & Schwabing**
Munich's upscale shopping boulevard (Maximilianstrasse) and the bohemian Schwabing neighborhood just north. Good for an afternoon walk, window shopping, and finding a good café. Less touristy than the Altstadt.

## Museums — Science, Technology & Engineering

**Deutsches Museum** ⭐ *recommended*
The world's largest science and technology museum — aviation, mining, chemistry, clocks, musical instruments, shipping, and more across multiple floors and buildings. The full main island museum is legitimately all-day (5–6 hours if you try). Both teens will find something to fixate on; the aviation hall and the mine shaft walk-through are perennial favorites. €15/adult, €4.50/youth (under 18).

**BMW Welt** ⭐ *recommended*
BMW's spectacular showroom/experience center next to the Olympic Park — it's free to enter and genuinely impressive architecturally. You can walk around concept cars, delivery bays, and interactive exhibits without buying anything. Feels more like a futuristic museum than a dealership. Often a highlight for teens even if they're not car enthusiasts.

**BMW Museum**
Right next to BMW Welt — the history of the brand from early motorcycles through modern M-series cars and a look at future mobility. Worth it if anyone in the family is interested in cars or design. €10/adult, €7/youth. Combine with BMW Welt as a half-day.

**Museum of Natural History (Paläontologisches Museum)**
Smaller and underrated — good dinosaur and fossil collection. Free admission. A low-key option for a shorter stop or if the Deutsches Museum feels overwhelming.

## Museums — History, Culture & Art

**NS-Dokumentationszentrum (Nazi Documentation Center)**
A serious, well-designed museum at the site of the original Nazi Party headquarters, walking through the rise of National Socialism in Munich specifically — because Munich was central to it. Not entertainment, but genuinely valuable context for the whole trip. Recommended for teens 14+ who can engage with it. €7/adult, free under 18. Pair with the walk past surrounding sites.

**Munich Residenz + Schatzkammer (Treasury)**
The former royal palace of the Bavarian Wittelsbach dynasty — opulent state rooms, a jaw-dropping treasury of crowns, reliquaries, and goldwork, and a beautiful antiquarium. It's close to the Hofgarten and easy to spend 2–3 hours. Interesting if the family is engaged by European monarchy history. €9/adult (combined ticket with treasury), free under 18.

**Jewish Museum Munich**
A thoughtful museum on the history of Jewish life in Munich from medieval times through the Nazi period to today. Well done and relatively compact — a 1.5-hour visit. Across from the Stadtmuseum on St.-Jakobs-Platz.

**Lenbachhaus**
Home of the Blue Rider (Blaue Reiter) collection — Kandinsky, Klee, Gabriele Münter, Marc. If anyone in the family likes modern or abstract art this is the best collection in Germany for that period. Otherwise, probably skip in favor of other stops.

**Alte Pinakothek**
One of Europe's finest collections of Old Masters (Rubens, Rembrandt, Dürer, Raphael). Beautiful building; serious art. Recommended only if the adults want it — teens may be indifferent unless they have an art interest.

**Münchner Stadtmuseum (City History Museum)**
The history of Munich itself — including a notable permanent collection on National Socialism. Also has temporary exhibits on Munich pop culture, music, and fashion. More accessible than it sounds. €4/adult, free under 18.

**Hunting and Fishing Museum (Jagd- und Fischereimuseum)**
A genuinely weird, fun detour — taxidermy, hunting weapons, fishing gear, and the Wolpertinger (Bavaria's mythical chimera creature). In a deconsecrated Augustinian church. It's deliberately quirky and teens often find it more amusing than expected. €4/adult, €2/youth.

## Palaces & Royal Sites

**Nymphenburg Palace & Gardens**
The summer palace of the Bavarian royal family — a long classical façade with formal French gardens, English landscape park, and smaller pavilions in the grounds (including the Amalienburg, a tiny rococo hunting lodge that's arguably more spectacular than the main palace). €15/adult, free under 18. Allow 2–3 hours for palace + gardens.

**Schloss Schleissheim**
Three palaces on one estate north of Munich, connected by Baroque gardens and canals — less visited than Nymphenburg, so much less crowded. The New Palace interior rivals Versailles in ambition if not quite in scale. A good full-morning option for palace fans who've already done Nymphenburg. Reachable by S-Bahn + short walk.

## Parks & Outdoors

**Englischer Garten** ⭐ *recommended*
Munich's massive English landscape park — bigger than Central Park in New York, running through the heart of the city. Walk, rent a bike, find a beer garden, or watch surfers ride the standing wave at the Eisbach inlet. In July the lawns fill with locals sunbathing; the park feels like the real Munich more than almost anywhere else in the city.

**Eisbach Surfers**
A man-made standing wave on the Eisbach river where the stream enters the Englischer Garten. Surfers queue up and take turns on a 10-second ride. Free to watch, completely unexpected in a landlocked city, and genuinely impressive. The 17-year-old may want to find out if walk-in surfing is possible (it sometimes is, with your own board).

**Isar River Gravel Bars (Flaucher area)**
South of the city center, the Isar has wide gravel beaches used by locals for swimming, picnicking, and barbecuing in summer. Not a tourist attraction — it's where Munich residents actually spend hot July days. The water runs cold and fast from the Alps. A good half-day option to feel like a local.

**Olympiapark**
The grounds from the 1972 Olympics are now a sprawling public park with a lake, concert venue, and the Olympic Tower. Free to walk around. The tent-roof architecture is genuinely distinctive and worth seeing from the outside. The Olympic Tower has an observation deck and revolving restaurant (€13/adult).

## Beer Gardens & Food Experiences

**Chinesischer Turm Beer Garden** ⭐ *recommended*
The most famous beer garden in the Englischer Garten — a thousand-seat garden around a Chinese pagoda tower, with a brass band playing most evenings in summer. Classic Munich atmosphere. Teens can order pretzels, Obatzda, and Radler (beer/lemon mix, low alcohol). Combining this with a park walk or bike ride makes a natural loop.

**Augustiner-Keller**
One of the oldest and most beloved beer gardens in the city — run by the Augustiner brewery, which many Münchners consider the best of the local beers. Less touristy than the Hofbräuhaus; more locals. The garden holds 5,000 people under chestnut trees. Very different vibe from Chinesischer Turm — bigger and more traditional.

**Hofbräuhaus**
The most famous beer hall in the world — loud, touristy, enormous, historic. Worth going at least once for the experience but temper expectations: it's packed with tourists in July, the service is harried, and regulars tend to have reservations in their own section. Fun as a bucket-list stop, not as a relaxed dinner.

**Viktualienmarkt Beer Garden**
A small beer garden embedded in the middle of the food market — no food sold here, you bring your own from the market stalls around it. Very local tradition. Good for a quick midday beer and snack in the sun.

**Munich Food Tour**
Several operators run 3-hour food tours through the Viktualienmarkt, Altstadt, and surrounding neighborhoods — pretzels, white sausage (Weisswurst), cheese, beer, Obatzda, and sweet mustard. Good orientation activity for Day 2 and gets the family oriented to local food. Look at Eating Europe or Context Travel for curated options.

**Weisswurst breakfast at a traditional café**
A Munich tradition: white veal sausages eaten with sweet mustard and a pretzel before noon (traditionally never eaten after midday). Worth doing once at a proper café — try Café Rischart on Marienplatz or any traditional Wirtschaft. €8–12 per person.

## Tours

**Mike's Bike Tours** ⭐ *specifically requested*
Munich's most popular guided bike tour — runs daily, about 4 hours, includes a beer garden lunch stop (Chinesischer Turm) mid-ride. Covers the Englischer Garten, royal buildings, the river, and more ground than any walking tour. The guides are typically young, energetic, and good with mixed-age groups; teens consistently rate this a highlight. Book ahead for July — it fills up. Classic tour ~€39/adult, teens pay less. mikesbiketours.com.

**Radius Tours — Third Reich Walking Tour**
A serious 2.5-hour walking tour of the historic center focusing on the sites connected to the rise of Nazism in Munich: the Bürgerbräukeller site, Nazi Party headquarters, the Feldherrnhalle, Dachau deportation context. Well-researched and appropriate for teens who engaged at the NS-Dokumentationszentrum. Different operators run variations of this; Radius Tours is well-regarded.

**Radius Tours — Dachau Concentration Camp Memorial** *(half-day trip, ~35 min by S-Bahn)*
Not technically within Munich but very accessible — the first Nazi concentration camp, now a memorial and museum 35 minutes by S-Bahn and bus. A difficult visit but an important one; most families who go are glad they did. Free admission; guided tour available. Would occupy a full half-day.

**Munich by boat — Isar / Nymphenburg canal**
A few operators run boat tours on the royal Nymphenburg canal or along the Isar in season. A lower-key option, more relaxing than informative. Good if the family wants something slower-paced.

## Active & Sports

**FC Bayern Munich — Allianz Arena Tour**
A behind-the-scenes stadium tour: the locker rooms, press area, tunnel, and pitch-side at the iconic inflatable-looking Allianz Arena. ~€17/adult, ~€10/youth. A good option for soccer fans even with no match scheduled. Combine with the BMW complex nearby for a full day north of the city.

**FC Bayern Match** *(long shot for July)*
The Bundesliga season ends in May and restarts in mid-August, so there are no regular season matches in July. Pre-season friendlies sometimes happen in late July at the Allianz Arena — worth checking as the date approaches.

**Olympic Tower Climb or Olympiapark Rooftop Walk**
The Olympiapark offers a "rooftop walk" along the famous tent-roof structure with a guide — a distinctive experience that involves scrambling across the canopy above the Olympic stadium. Not for those afraid of heights; very popular with teens. Book well ahead. Separate from the standard observation tower. ~€55/person.

**Indoor Climbing at DAV Kletterzentrum**
Munich has an excellent climbing gym run by the German Alpine Club (DAV) — good for an active half-day if the family is into climbing or wants to try it. Several walls including beginner routes. Rental gear available. A good option on a rainy afternoon.

**Isar River Rafting (Floßfahrt)** *(seasonal, typically May–August)*
Traditional Bavarian log-raft rides down a stretch of the Isar — wooden rafts, a brass band, beer and food on board, several hours on the river. Extremely popular with locals and a genuine Munich tradition. Book well ahead; July slots fill months in advance. Departs from Wolfratshausen; ends in Munich. ~€50–80/person depending on operator.

**Surfing the Eisbach** *(for the 17-year-old)*
If the 17-year-old surfs at all, the Eisbach is a genuine surf spot — a fast, powerful standing wave that experts ride in a few seconds. Walk-ups with their own boards are sometimes allowed at off-peak times, though the etiquette is strict and it's harder than it looks. Worth checking before the trip.

## July Events & Seasonal

**Tollwood Summer Festival — Olympiapark** ⭐ *recommended*
A large outdoor arts, music, food, and culture festival running from late June through the last week of July in the Olympic Park. Free entry to the grounds; food stalls, craft vendors, live music across multiple stages, circus acts, and evening headliner concerts (ticketed). One of the best things happening in Munich in July — casual, cosmopolitan, and unlike anything touristy. Perfect for an evening out.

**Kino, Mond und Sterne (Open-Air Cinema)**
Munich hosts several open-air cinema series in summer — films screened at the Olympiapark lake, the English Garden, and other venues. Films often in original language with German subtitles. Good low-key evening option. Dates vary by year; check what's playing in the week you're there.

**Residenz Summer Concert Series**
Classical concerts held in the Residenz's ornate Cuvilliés Theatre or the Brunnenhof courtyard — a chance to hear chamber music in an 18th-century setting. Program varies; tickets typically €20–40. A good option for one evening if classical music appeals.

**Klassik am Odeonsplatz**
A free outdoor classical concert sometimes held at Odeonsplatz in summer — huge audience, Munich Philharmonic or similar ensemble, festive atmosphere. Dates aren't always fixed a year ahead; worth checking as July approaches.

## Quirky & Off-the-Beaten-Path

**Maximilianeum & Max-II-Denkmal**
A dramatic Neo-Gothic building on a hill across the Isar housing the Bavarian State Parliament — the exterior and the boulevard leading to it are impressive for a 20-minute walk. The street itself (Maximilianstrasse) transitions from luxury shops to more residential as you cross the bridge.

**Hellabrun Zoo (Tierpark Hellabrunn)**
A well-regarded zoo along the Isar, notable for being one of the first "geo-zoos" organized by animal's native geography rather than species type. Good for a relaxed morning if any family member is into animals. €20/adult, €9/youth.

**Augustiner Beer Hall (not the Keller/garden — the indoor hall)**
The formal Augustiner beer hall in the Altstadt is a proper 19th-century Munich institution — vaulted ceilings, less rowdy than the Hofbräuhaus, more local atmosphere. Easier to get a table than the Hofbräuhaus and the food is better.

**Walking the Kunstareal**
Munich's "museum quarter" concentrated around the three Pinakotheken galleries — even if you don't go inside, the Maxvorstadt neighborhood around it has good architecture, independent cafés, and a young local crowd. A good evening stroll area.
