# Day Trips from Munich
*Family of 4 · teens 17 & 14 · July 2026 · All reachable by train or bus — no rental car needed*

---

## Castles

**Neuschwanstein Castle** ⭐ *already in itinerary*
The fairy-tale castle commissioned by King Ludwig II — perched on a rocky crag above the Alpsee lake, and the direct inspiration for the Disney castle logo. The interior timed tour (~35 min) covers the throne room, singers' hall, and Ludwig's famously unfinished private rooms. Combine with the Marienbrücke suspension bridge above the castle for the iconic elevated view, and optionally Hohenschwangau Castle (Ludwig's childhood home) in the valley below.
- **Getting there:** Train to Füssen (~2 hrs from Munich Hbf, regional express); bus 73 or 78 to castle base; uphill walk or horse carriage to entrance
- **Booking:** Timed interior tours sell out weeks ahead in July — book immediately at tickets.hohenschwangau.de. Marienbrücke is free and no booking needed.
- **Duration:** Full day (leave Munich by 8am; back by 7pm)
- **Cost:** ~€15/adult, €9/youth for Neuschwanstein interior; add ~€20/adult for Hohenschwangau

**Hohenschwangau Castle** *(pair with Neuschwanstein)*
Ludwig II grew up here — this is his father Maximilian II's castle, less famous but arguably more intimate. The interior is more liveable in scale than Neuschwanstein and gives good context for Ludwig's obsessions (Wagner, medieval German legend, swans). Walking distance from Neuschwanstein; same ticket hub at the base of the hill.
- **Note:** If time is tight, Neuschwanstein is the priority. But the combination makes for a richer day.

**Nördlingen** *(castle town + meteor crater)*
A medieval walled town sitting inside a 15-million-year-old meteor impact crater — the entire town is ringed by a walkable original city wall. The Daniel Tower (90m) gives panoramic views of the crater rim, which you can literally see from the top. Smaller and less visited than Rothenburg; a more authentic feel. Good if the family wants a medieval town without the crowds.
- **Getting there:** Train to Nördlingen (~1.5–2 hrs via Donauwörth); no direct ICE, regional connections
- **Duration:** Half to full day
- **Cost:** Daniel Tower €3/adult, €1.50/youth; most of the town itself is free to walk

**Burghausen Castle** *(longest castle complex in the world)*
A castle that stretches nearly a kilometer along a ridge above the Salzach river — six successive courtyards connected by towers and gates. Almost entirely unvisited by international tourists despite being genuinely spectacular. The town below it is charming Austrian-influenced Bavarian, and the castle hosts a jazz festival in summer that sometimes overlaps with July dates.
- **Getting there:** Train from Munich Hbf (~1.5 hrs via Mühldorf); some connections require a change
- **Duration:** Half to full day
- **Cost:** €5/adult, free under 18

**Landsberg am Lech** *(walled town + Romantic Road)*
A very well-preserved medieval town on the Lech river, on the old Romantic Road. Less visited than Rothenburg ob der Tauber but has the same medieval gates, towers, and market square. The Bayertor (Bavarian Gate) is one of the finest Gothic town gates in Germany. Good for a half-day if the family wants a quick medieval town fix closer to Munich.
- **Getting there:** Train from Munich Hbf (~45 min direct); very easy
- **Duration:** Half day
- **Cost:** Free town, minor entry fees for specific sites

**Rothenburg ob der Tauber** *(the iconic medieval walled town)*
The most-photographed medieval town in Germany — half-timbered houses, a fully intact city wall you can walk on top of, and a Christmas market that runs year-round. Very touristy in July but genuinely beautiful. The Night Watchman tour is one of the best guided tours in Europe. Worth it if the family is drawn to the storybook medieval aesthetic.
- **Getting there:** Train from Munich Hbf (~2.5–3 hrs via Ansbach or Steinach; connections required)
- **Duration:** Full day
- **Cost:** City wall free; Night Watchman tour ~€9/adult

## Alpine & Lakes

**Zugspitze — Germany's Highest Peak**
The summit of Germany at 2,962m — you ride a cog railway from Garmisch-Partenkirchen to near the top, then a cable car to the summit. On a clear day you can see into four countries. There's a summit restaurant, a glacier walk, and the border between Germany and Austria runs right across the peak. The most dramatic mountain experience reachable as a day trip; genuinely memorable if the weather cooperates.
- **Getting there:** Train to Garmisch-Partenkirchen (~1.5 hrs from Munich Hbf); then cog railway + cable car
- **Booking:** Buy tickets in advance online; July is busy. zugspitze.de
- **Duration:** Full day (leave Munich by 7:30am)
- **Cost:** ~€68/adult, ~€37/youth for the combined cog railway + cable car round trip
- **Weather caveat:** Check the forecast carefully — summit can be socked in with cloud. Have a backup plan.

**Garmisch-Partenkirchen + Partnachklamm Gorge**
Even without going up the Zugspitze, Garmisch is a lovely Alpine town worth visiting. The Partnachklamm is a narrow river gorge carved through the rock — a 45-minute walk through dripping cliffs, waterfalls, and carved-out path sections. Dramatic and accessible, and can be wet even in summer (bring a light rain layer). Pair with lunch in Garmisch town.
- **Getting there:** Train to Garmisch (~1.5 hrs)
- **Duration:** Half to full day
- **Cost:** Gorge entry ~€5/adult, €2/youth

**Starnberger See (Lake Starnberg)**
The closest lake to Munich — 25 minutes by S-Bahn. In July it's full of locals swimming, sailing, and cycling the lakeside path. The town of Starnberg has a promenade, boat rentals, and cafés. Good for a relaxed afternoon if the family needs a slower day. King Ludwig II drowned here under mysterious circumstances in 1886; there's a memorial cross in the lake.
- **Getting there:** S6 from Munich Hbf to Starnberg (~25 min)
- **Duration:** Half day or leisurely full day
- **Cost:** Mostly free; boat rentals ~€15–25/hr

**Chiemsee — "The Bavarian Sea"**
Bavaria's largest lake with a ferry connecting three islands — including Herreninsel, where King Ludwig II built an unfinished replica of Versailles (Herrenchiemsee Palace). The palace is genuinely extraordinary — the Hall of Mirrors rivals the original and because it's far less visited, you can actually enjoy it. Combine with a swim at the lake, a walk around Fraueninsel (the nuns' island), and lunch at a lakeside café.
- **Getting there:** Train to Prien am Chiemsee (~1 hr from Munich Hbf); then ferry to islands
- **Duration:** Full day
- **Cost:** ~€12/adult ferry round trip to Herreninsel; palace entry ~€10/adult, free under 18

**Königssee** *(already in Berchtesgaden segment)*
The most beautiful lake in Germany — surrounded by nearly vertical cliff walls, accessible only by electric boat. Already in the itinerary as part of the Berchtesgaden overnight. Mentioned here for completeness.

## History

**Dachau Concentration Camp Memorial**
The first Nazi concentration camp, 35 minutes by S-Bahn and bus from central Munich. Now a memorial and museum — the original roll-call square, reconstructed barracks, crematoria, and a thorough exhibition on the camp's history from 1933 to 1945. A difficult visit but an important one; families who do it are almost universally glad they did, and teens take it seriously in a way that's hard to replicate anywhere else. Free admission. Guided tours available (book ahead).
- **Getting there:** S2 from Munich Hbf to Dachau (20 min); bus 726 to the memorial (12 min)
- **Duration:** Half day (3–4 hrs)
- **Cost:** Free; guided tour ~€3/person via KZ-Gedenkstätte Dachau

**Augsburg**
Bavaria's second oldest city after Trier — founded by the Romans, home to some of the finest Renaissance architecture in Germany. The Fuggerei is the world's oldest social housing complex (still inhabited, still charging the same rent as 1521: 88 cents/year plus three daily prayers). The Golden Hall of the Town Hall is extraordinary. Less visited than it deserves; a manageable half-day or full day.
- **Getting there:** Train from Munich Hbf (~35–45 min direct)
- **Duration:** Half to full day
- **Cost:** Fuggerei entry ~€8/adult, €4/youth; most of the city is free

## Quirky / Wild Card

**Andechs Monastery + Brewery**
A Benedictine monastery on a hilltop above the Ammersee lake — pilgrimages have happened here for over 600 years, and the monks still brew excellent beer on site. The monastery beer garden and restaurant are genuinely good, and the church interior is rococo at its most exuberant. A peaceful, scenic half-day that combines culture, history, and very good food and beer.
- **Getting there:** S8 to Herrsching (~1 hr), then hike 5 km uphill (lovely) or bus
- **Duration:** Half day
- **Cost:** Free entry to church and grounds; food/beer at the restaurant

**Oberammergau**
A small Alpine village famous for its Passion Play (held every 10 years, next in 2030) and for wood-carving tradition — every other building seems to be a carving workshop or painted with elaborate Lüftlmalerei frescoes. The village is genuinely charming and the surrounding Alps make it a good base for a short hike. Pairs well with Linderhof Palace (a short bus ride away — another Ludwig II castle, more intimate than Neuschwanstein).
- **Getting there:** Train to Murnau + bus (~2 hrs total)
- **Duration:** Half to full day if combining with Linderhof

**Linderhof Palace** *(Ludwig II's intimate retreat)*
The smallest of Ludwig II's three castles and the only one he actually lived in and completed. Set in a forested valley in the Alps with formal French gardens, a Venus Grotto (an artificial cave with a boat and colored lighting inspired by Wagner's Tannhäuser), and a Moorish Kiosk. More personal and less overwhelming than Neuschwanstein. A good pairing with Oberammergau.
- **Getting there:** Train to Oberammergau + bus to Linderhof (~2–2.5 hrs total)
- **Duration:** 2–3 hrs at the palace
- **Cost:** ~€12/adult, free under 18
